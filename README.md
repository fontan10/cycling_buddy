# Cycling Buddy

**A child-centered platform for reporting cycling infrastructure issues — designed by and for young cyclists.**

Cycling Buddy puts children at the heart of urban mobility research. It gives kids a fast, enjoyable way to flag problems they encounter while cycling — potholes, scary crossings, broken bike parking, poor lighting — and turns their lived experience into structured, mappable data that communities and researchers can act on.

---

## Why This Matters

Children are among the most vulnerable road users, yet their perspective is almost entirely absent from cycling infrastructure planning. Adults design bike lanes and parking facilities; children use them — and notice things adults miss.

Cycling Buddy bridges that gap. By giving young cyclists a voice and a simple tool to use in the moment, the platform generates **ground-truth data on children's cycling experience** that would otherwise never be captured. Every report is a data point: a GPS coordinate, a category, a photo, a description — contributed by someone who actually rides that route.

For researchers studying active travel, school-trip safety, urban child mobility, or participatory planning, this is a new kind of evidence base.

---

## What the App Does

1. **Spot a problem** — a child selects a category tile that matches what they encountered (broken path, scary junction, hazardous road surface, etc.)
2. **Describe it** — add a location, a short description, and an optional photo
3. **Submit** — the report is logged, pinned to a community map, and attributed to the child's team
4. **See the impact** — reports appear on a live map; teams earn points for contributing, creating positive reinforcement for civic participation

The experience is intentionally fast and low-friction. A report can be filed in under a minute, making it realistic for children to report in the moment rather than remembering later.

---

## Issue Categories

Reports are structured around five categories that reflect how children themselves describe cycling problems:

| Category | What it captures |
|---|---|
| **Tricky to Get Around** | Missing parking, blocked paths, absent signage |
| **Broken or Missing** | Damaged surfaces, broken lights, missing infrastructure |
| **Felt Scary** | Dangerous junctions, traffic too close, poor visibility |
| **Road Hazard** | Potholes, construction, unsafe road design |
| **Accident or Close Call** | Near-misses and collisions |

The category language is deliberately written from a child's point of view — "felt scary" rather than "perceived risk" — to lower the barrier to reporting and capture experiences adults might not think to ask about.

## Key Features

### Community Map

All reports appear on an interactive map with clustering, making problem hotspots immediately visible. Researchers and planners can see at a glance where children experience the most difficulty.

### Team-Based Participation

Children join teams (typically a school class or cycling club) and earn points for every report they file. A leaderboard encourages friendly competition while building a culture of civic engagement.

### Structured Data

Every report captures GPS coordinates, category, subcategory, free-text description, and optional photo — making the dataset suitable for spatial analysis, trend identification, and longitudinal tracking.

### Child-Centered Design

The interface follows the **Tactile Playground** design system — large rounded tap targets suited to developing motor skills, high-contrast typography, and a warm visual aesthetic inspired by physical wooden toys. The goal is an app that children actually enjoy using.

## Research Potential

Cycling Buddy is built for participatory action research with children. Potential applications include:

- **Spatial analysis** of cycling hazard distribution across neighbourhoods
- **Longitudinal tracking** of infrastructure improvements (or deterioration) over time
- **Comparative studies** across schools, age groups, or urban/suburban contexts
- **Qualitative research** using children's own descriptions and photos as primary data
- **Engagement research** on gamification as a tool for youth civic participation
- **Policy evidence** for local authorities planning cycling infrastructure investment

The platform is designed to complement existing methods (surveys, interviews, ride-alongs) by providing continuous, in-situ data collection at scale.

---

## Design Philosophy

The UI is built around the principle that **friction is a barrier to participation**. If reporting feels like a task, children won't do it. If it feels like a game, they will.

Every design decision — the large bubble buttons, the team leaderboard, the immediate visual feedback on submission — is aimed at making reporting habitual and rewarding rather than occasional and effortful.
